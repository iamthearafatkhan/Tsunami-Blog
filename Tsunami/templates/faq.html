Tsunami Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

A tsunami is a series of large ocean waves typically caused by seismic events such as earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or underwater landslides.

Tsunamis are primarily caused by underwater earthquakes, but they can also result from volcanic eruptions, landslides, or the impact of meteorites in the ocean.

Tsunamis can travel at speeds of up to 500 to 800 km/h (310 to 500 mph) in deep water, but they slow down as they approach shallow coastal areas.

If you receive a tsunami warning, evacuate to higher ground immediately and follow the instructions from local authorities.

While tsunamis primarily affect coastal areas, they can travel up rivers and into inland regions, causing destruction far from the ocean.

Prepare an emergency kit, develop an evacuation plan, and familiarize yourself with local tsunami evacuation routes.

A tsunami watch means a tsunami is possible, while a warning indicates that a tsunami is imminent or already occurring.

Tsunamis occur regularly around the world, but large and destructive tsunamis are relatively rare, happening only a few times a century.

Some studies suggest that animals may be able to sense changes in the environment before a tsunami occurs, potentially allowing them to flee to safety.

Tsunamis can cause significant destruction to coastal ecosystems, including coral reefs, mangroves, and marine habitats, along with erosion and sediment displacement.
